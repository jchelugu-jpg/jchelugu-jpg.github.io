    Taysha

I first realised that I was experiencing mental illness when I was constantly feeling like everything I did wasn't good enough.

Diagnosed with Depression, Anxiety, and later PTSD starting at age 15, I initally felt that setbacks were a sign of faliure. I felt relieved when I got my diagnosis because I felt as though I could finally work towards managing my mental health. However, I was angry and frustrated after my diagnois of PTSD. I had been working hard for a year getting my depression and anxiety under control and I was making progress, so it felt like a step backwards.

    Elliot

I remember vividly at school in Year 5 playing on the monkey bars and thinking to myself, "I just want to live a life where I'm not nervous all the time"

Quite the profound thought for a kid in Primary School

Once I got a bit older, around Year 10 it became apparent that something was holding me back, although back then mental health wasn't really something spoken about. I assumed it was only me who felt these feelings. I was first diagnosed with Depression and Generalized Anxiety Disorder. I was prescribied medication, told to excercise, get plenty of sleep, and to see a psychologist. In he interim, it validated my feelings. That was important. You cannot defeat something when you don't know what you're dealing with. So, I looked into the different treatment options and how to gain access. I had regular appointments with my GP and together we got a Mental Health Care Plan sorted so I could see a psychologist regulary. Most importantly, I set some goals of what I wanted to gain from treatment. Friendships are also key. I am blessed to have such great friend's circle where we can help manage each other's burdens as needed. They might not know exactly what I'm dealing with, but they don't need to. The fact that I know they will be there for me if things go bad again is comfort enough, and of course I would do the same for them. Life isn't easy, for anybody.
