THE BETTLE

External Morphology

Head

The head of the bettle consists of mouthparts projecting forward or sometimes downturned. The eyes on the bettle are compound, the eyes are split to allow a view both above and below the waterline.

Mouthparts

The complex structure of the bettles mouth has several parts including an upper lip, a pair of powerful jaws for crushing food. A pair of more complex apendages are used for manipulating food and gathering liquids.

Thorax

The middle, armored body section, located between the head and abdomen. This serves as the attachement point for all six legs and both pairs of wings.

Prothrorax and Pterothorax

The thorax is segmented into two discernible parts, the pro- and pterothorax. The pterothorax is the fused meso- and metathorax, which is commonly seperated in other insect species.

Legs

The multisegmented legs end in two to five small segments called tarsi. Similar to many other insects, bettles have claws, usually one pair; on the end of the last tarsal segment of each leg.

Wings

The forewings of beetles are not used for flight, but they form elytra which cover the hind part of the body and protect the hindwings. The elytra is usually a hard shell-like structure - this allows the hindwings to move for flight.

Abdomen

The abdomen of the bettle is the section behind the metathorax which is made up of a series of rings. The abdomen consits of holes each for breathing and respiration called a spiracle.
